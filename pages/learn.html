📚 Learn About Brain Health

Clear, compassionate education on the neurological conditions that matter most — written for real people, not doctors.

Alzheimer's Disease

Alzheimer's is a progressive brain disorder that slowly destroys memory, thinking, and eventually the ability to carry out simple tasks. It is the most common cause of dementia, accounting for 60–80% of cases.

🔬 What Happens in the Brain?

Abnormal protein clumps called "plaques" and "tangles" build up between and within nerve cells. This disrupts communication between neurons and eventually causes cells to die.

📅 Progression

Alzheimer's progresses through early, middle, and late stages — typically over 8 to 10 years. Early stages involve mild forgetfulness; late stages require full-time care.

🔢 By the Numbers

Over 6.9 million Americans over 65 live with Alzheimer's. The risk doubles every 5 years after age 65. About 1 in 3 seniors dies with Alzheimer's or another dementia.

💊 Treatment Today

There is no cure, but FDA-approved medications like lecanemab can slow progression in early stages. Lifestyle modifications, cognitive training, and social engagement also help.

🛡️ Prevention Tips

Regular aerobic exercise, a Mediterranean diet, quality sleep, mental stimulation, and managing blood pressure and diabetes all reduce Alzheimer's risk.

🧬 Genetics

The APOE-e4 gene increases risk but doesn't guarantee the disease. Only about 1% of cases are caused by rare gene mutations passed down in families (early-onset Alzheimer's).

⚠️ 10 Warning Signs of Alzheimer's

  • Memory loss that disrupts daily life (forgetting recently learned information)
  • Challenges in planning or solving problems
  • Difficulty completing familiar tasks at home, work, or leisure
  • Confusion with time or place
  • Trouble understanding visual images and spatial relationships
  • New problems with words in speaking or writing
  • Misplacing things and inability to retrace steps
  • Decreased or poor judgment (e.g., being scammed more easily)
  • Withdrawal from work or social activities
  • Changes in mood and personality

Parkinson's Disease

Parkinson's is a progressive nervous system disorder that primarily affects movement. It develops when neurons in a brain region called the substantia nigra break down or die, reducing the production of dopamine.

🔬 The Dopamine Connection

Dopamine is the brain's "movement messenger." When dopamine-producing cells die, signals between the brain and muscles become slow and imprecise, causing the hallmark tremors and rigidity.

🚶 Motor vs. Non-Motor Symptoms

While tremors, stiffness, and slow movement are well-known, Parkinson's also causes non-motor symptoms: depression, sleep problems, loss of smell, and cognitive changes.

📋 Who Gets It?

About 90,000 new cases are diagnosed in the US each year. It typically begins after age 60, but young-onset Parkinson's (before 50) affects about 10–20% of patients.

💊 Levodopa

The most effective medication is levodopa, which the brain converts to dopamine. Physical therapy, occupational therapy, and deep brain stimulation (DBS) are also important treatments.

🏃 Exercise is Medicine

Research shows that vigorous aerobic exercise — especially boxing, cycling, and tango dancing — can significantly slow Parkinson's progression by promoting neuroplasticity.

🌙 Sleep & Parkinson's

REM sleep behavior disorder — where people act out vivid dreams — is one of the earliest warning signs of Parkinson's, sometimes preceding motor symptoms by 10+ years.

⚠️ Early Warning Signs

  • Tremor in a finger, thumb, hand, or chin while at rest
  • Smaller handwriting than usual (micrographia)
  • Loss of smell (hyposmia) — especially for things like bananas or licorice
  • Trouble sleeping — thrashing or acting out dreams
  • Stiffness in arms, legs, or trunk that doesn't go away when moving
  • Constipation with no clear cause
  • Softer, lower, or hoarse voice
  • Masked face — a serious, depressed, or mad look even when not feeling that way

Dementia

Dementia is not a single disease — it's an umbrella term for a group of symptoms affecting memory, thinking, and social abilities severely enough to interfere with daily life. Alzheimer's is the most common type, but there are many others.

🌫️ Types of Dementia

Major types include: Alzheimer's disease (60–80%), Vascular dementia (after strokes), Lewy body dementia, Frontotemporal dementia, and mixed dementia (more than one type at once).

🆚 Normal Aging vs. Dementia

Occasionally forgetting a name but remembering it later is normal aging. Forgetting important events, getting lost in familiar places, and asking the same question repeatedly are warning signs.

🩺 Reversible Causes

Some conditions can mimic dementia and are treatable: thyroid problems, vitamin B12 deficiency, depression, medication side effects, and urinary tract infections in older adults.

🌐 Global Impact

55 million people worldwide live with dementia. A new case is diagnosed every 3 seconds globally. Dementia is one of the leading causes of disability among older adults worldwide.

🧠 The MIND Diet

The MIND diet (Mediterranean-DASH Intervention for Neurodegenerative Delay) combines two proven diets and has been shown to reduce dementia risk by up to 53% in strict followers.

❤️ Vascular Dementia

The second most common type is caused by reduced blood flow to the brain — often from a series of small strokes. Controlling blood pressure and cholesterol is key prevention.

⚠️ Seven Stages of Dementia

  • Stage 1: No impairment — normal function
  • Stage 2: Very mild decline — normal forgetfulness
  • Stage 3: Mild decline — family members may notice problems
  • Stage 4: Moderate decline — difficulty with complex tasks, may withdraw socially
  • Stage 5: Moderately severe — needs help with daily activities, may forget address or major current events
  • Stage 6: Severe — needs extensive help, may not recognize spouse or children
  • Stage 7: Very severe — loss of ability to speak and walk

Stroke

A stroke occurs when blood supply to part of the brain is cut off. Without oxygen, brain cells begin dying within minutes. Stroke is a medical emergency — fast treatment is essential.

🔴 Ischemic vs. Hemorrhagic

87% of strokes are ischemic (a clot blocks a blood vessel). The remaining 13% are hemorrhagic (a blood vessel ruptures). Both are emergencies requiring different treatments.

⚡ The BE-FAST Test

Balance, Eyes, Face drooping, Arm weakness, Speech difficulty, Time to call 911. Learn these — they can save a life.

⏰ Time is Brain

Every minute during a stroke, about 1.9 million neurons die. The clot-busting drug tPA must be given within 4.5 hours of symptom onset — which is why BE-FAST awareness matters.

🔄 TIA — The Warning Stroke

A transient ischemic attack (TIA or "mini-stroke") causes temporary symptoms lasting minutes to hours. It is a major warning sign — 1 in 10 TIA patients has a full stroke within 3 months.

🏋️ Recovery & Rehab

The brain is remarkably adaptable. Physical, speech, and occupational therapy help the brain form new pathways. Most recovery happens in the first 3–6 months after stroke.

🛡️ 80% Preventable

Up to 80% of strokes are preventable. Key steps: control blood pressure, stop smoking, manage diabetes, maintain healthy weight, limit alcohol, and treat atrial fibrillation.

⚠️ Remember BE-FAST

  • B — Balance: Sudden loss of balance or coordination
  • E — Eyes: Sudden vision changes in one or both eyes
  • F — Face: Ask person to smile — does one side droop?
  • A — Arms: Ask person to raise both arms — does one drift down?
  • S — Speech: Ask person to repeat a simple sentence — is speech slurred or strange?
  • T — Time: If ANY of these are present, call 911 IMMEDIATELY

Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI)

MCI is the stage between normal age-related cognitive changes and the more serious decline of dementia. People with MCI have noticeable changes in memory or thinking, but can still live independently.

🔍 What is MCI?

MCI causes cognitive changes noticeable to the person and others, but not severe enough to interfere significantly with daily life. It affects about 15–20% of people over 65.

🔀 Two Types

Amnestic MCI (the most common type) primarily affects memory. Non-amnestic MCI affects other thinking skills like language, attention, or visual-spatial ability.

📊 What Happens Next?

About 10–15% of people with MCI go on to develop dementia each year. However, some people stay stable, and some even improve — especially when a reversible cause is found.

🔬 Diagnosis

Doctors use cognitive tests, brain imaging (MRI), blood tests, and sometimes cerebrospinal fluid analysis to distinguish MCI from normal aging or early dementia.

💪 What You Can Do

MCI is a crucial intervention window. Regular exercise, cognitive training, social engagement, treating sleep apnea, and managing vascular risk factors all reduce conversion to dementia.

🧪 Emerging Research

New blood tests can detect amyloid beta — the hallmark of Alzheimer's — years before symptoms appear, opening doors for very early intervention during the MCI stage.

⚠️ Signs That May Indicate MCI

  • Forgetting things more often than you used to
  • Forgetting important events such as appointments or social engagements
  • Losing your train of thought or the thread of conversations, books, or movies
  • Feeling increasingly overwhelmed by making decisions or planning steps
  • Having trouble finding your way around familiar places
  • Your family or friends notice memory slips or personality changes
  • Feeling more anxious, depressed, or irritable than usual
