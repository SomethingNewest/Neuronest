🤝 Caregiver Corner

For the families, partners, and friends who show up every day. You're not alone — and you matter too.

A note to caregivers

Caring for someone with a neurological condition is one of the most demanding — and meaningful — roles a person can fill. This section is dedicated to you. Here you'll find guidance on understanding symptoms, communicating effectively, recognizing when to get more help, and taking care of yourself, too.

🗣️ Communication Tips

  • Speak slowly, clearly, and in short sentences
  • Use their name at the beginning of sentences to get attention
  • Ask one question at a time — avoid giving multiple choices
  • Don't correct or argue — redirect instead
  • Use gestures, pictures, or written notes when words aren't enough
  • Approach from the front, make eye contact, and smile
  • Give extra time to respond — don't rush or finish sentences for them

⚠️ Red Flags — When to See a Doctor Immediately

  • Sudden, dramatic change in personality or behavior
  • New difficulty walking or increased falls
  • Sudden confusion or inability to recognize familiar people
  • Signs of a stroke (BE-FAST: Balance, Eyes, Face, Arms, Speech, Time)
  • Expressions of wanting to harm themselves
  • Sudden inability to swallow or speak
  • High fever combined with confusion (could indicate infection)

🏡 Creating a Safe Home

  • Remove loose rugs and clutter from walkways to prevent falls
  • Install grab bars in bathrooms near the toilet and shower
  • Use nightlights throughout the home for nighttime navigation
  • Lock away medications, cleaning chemicals, and sharp objects
  • Put labels on important doors and drawers (e.g., "BATHROOM")
  • Consider door alarms if wandering is a concern
  • Simplify the environment — reduce visual clutter and distractions

🧘 Caregiver Self-Care

  • Caregiver burnout is real — watch for signs like exhaustion, resentment, or depression
  • Accept help from others — you cannot do this alone
  • Join a caregiver support group (in-person or online)
  • Use respite care services for temporary relief
  • Maintain your own medical appointments and health checkups
  • Set boundaries — caring for yourself is not selfish, it's necessary
  • Give yourself permission to grieve — caring for someone with dementia involves ongoing loss

💊 Medication Management

  • Use a pill organizer or blister packs to prevent double-dosing
  • Keep a written medication list with dosages and times
  • Bring the full medication list to every doctor's appointment
  • Watch for side effects — many common medications affect cognition in older adults
  • Ask the pharmacist to review for drug interactions regularly
  • Some medications for anxiety or sleep can worsen cognitive symptoms — ask the doctor

📋 Legal & Financial Planning

  • Set up a durable power of attorney while your loved one can still participate in the decision
  • Establish healthcare proxy / medical power of attorney documents
  • Create or update an advance directive / living will
  • Review beneficiary designations on financial accounts and insurance
  • Consult an elder law attorney — many specialize in dementia planning
  • Understand Medicaid and Medicare coverage for memory care and long-term care

What to Expect at Each Stage

🌱 Early Stage

Your loved one is likely still largely independent. Focus on legal planning, safety modifications, establishing routines, and encouraging continued hobbies and social activities.

🌤️ Middle Stage

Increased supervision is needed. Help with daily tasks like dressing and meals. Wandering and behavioral changes may emerge. This is often when in-home care or adult day programs become helpful.

🌙 Late Stage

Full-time care is required. Focus shifts to comfort, dignity, and quality of life. Hospice services may become appropriate. Grief support for the whole family is important at this stage.
